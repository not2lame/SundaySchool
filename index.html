Background: A New and Glorious Doctrine in Nauvoo

The revelations in this lesson were given during the Nauvoo period, a time of both intense trial and profound doctrinal restoration. The Prophet Joseph Smith was once again facing persecution and false accusations, forcing him into hiding in August 1842. Despite these "deep waters," he continued to lead the Church through inspired letters.

A central theme of this period was the public introduction of the doctrine of baptism for the dead. While the principle had been revealed to the Prophet earlier, he first taught it publicly at the funeral of a man named Seymour Brunson in August 1840. The Saints, many of whom were grieving loved ones who had died without the gospel, received this doctrine with immense joy. As Vilate Kimball wrote to her husband, Heber, "President Smith has opened a new and glorious subject which has caused quite a revival in the church." This revelation brought hope for ancestors and created a "welding link" between the generations, fundamentally shaping the temple-focused work of the Church today.

Introduction: Hope for a Grieving Mother

In August 1840, a grieving mother named Jane Neyman listened to the Prophet Joseph speak at a funeral. Her own teenage son, Cyrus, had recently passed away without being baptized, and Jane was deeply worried about his eternal soul. Joseph Smith understood this pain intimately, having felt the same sorrow for his beloved brother Alvin.

At that funeral, the Prophet publicly shared for the first time what the Lord had revealed to him: that we can perform saving ordinances, like baptism, for our deceased ancestors. This doctrine thrilled the Saints! A wave of hope and joy swept through the community as their thoughts turned to their own loved ones who had died without the gospel. Joseph shared their joy, later writing with enthusiastic language about this "voice of gladness for the living and the dead," exclaiming, ‚ÄúLet the mountains shout for joy, and all ye valleys cry aloud!‚Äù (Doctrine and Covenants 128:23).

The Lord wants me to care for my family.

Discussion Point:

After years of near-constant missionary service that required immense sacrifice from his family, Through the prophet Joseph Smith, Brigham Young received a personal revelation. The Lord released him from further travel abroad, telling him, "it is no more required at your hand," and commanded him to "take especial care of your family." This revelation teaches a powerful principle about seasons of service and the Lord's concern for the family unit.

Doctrine and Covenants 126:3: "I therefore command you to send my word abroad, and take especial care of your family from this time, henceforth and forever. Amen."

Discussion Question:

The Lord honors sacrifice in His service, yet He also commanded Brigham Young to focus on his family. How can we find balance in our own lives between our Church callings, work responsibilities, and the command to "take especial care" of our families?

Possible Answers: Finding balance requires seeking personal revelation. It means recognizing that what the Lord requires of us can change in different seasons of life. It involves counseling with our spouse and family, being fully present where we are, and trusting that the Lord will consecrate our sincere efforts both at home and in our callings.

I can rely on the Lord during difficult times.

Discussion Point:

Joseph Smith wrote the letter that became Section 127 while in hiding from those who were unjustly seeking his life. Despite the danger and turmoil, his words are not fearful or bitter, but full of faith and optimism. He declares that he glories in tribulation, trusting that God will deliver him. His example teaches us that our inner peace does not have to be dependent on our outward circumstances.

Doctrine and Covenants 127:2: "Deep water is what I am wont to swim in. It all has become a second nature to me... I feel like Paul, to glory in tribulation; for to this day has the God of my fathers delivered me out of them all..."

Discussion Question:

Joseph Smith was able to feel joy and gratitude even while in "deep water." What principles from these verses can help us find peace and rely on the Lord when we face our own trials and opposition?

Possible Answers: Joseph focused on the love of God and his friends, which outweighed his perils. He remembered past deliverances and trusted God would help him again. He found joy in the work of the kingdom. We can likewise find peace by focusing on our blessings, counting on God's love, and staying engaged in His service, which lifts our perspective above our immediate troubles.

"Whatsoever you record on earth shall be recorded in heaven."

Discussion Point:

The Lord gave very specific instructions about the practice of baptism for the dead. He commanded that a recorder be present, that witnesses observe the ordinance, and that everything be carefully documented in the records of the Church. This is because the earthly record must mirror the records kept in heaven. This principle emphasizes the sacred and orderly nature of God's work.

Doctrine and Covenants 128:8: "Now, the nature of this ordinance consists in the power of the priesthood, by the revelation of Jesus Christ, wherein it is granted that whatsoever you bind on earth shall be bound in heaven, and whatsoever you loose on earth shall be loosed in heaven."

Discussion Question:

The Lord places great importance on the official recording of saving ordinances. How might this principle of careful record-keeping apply to our own personal and family records, such as journals and family histories?

Possible Answers: Our personal and family histories are, in a sense, our own "book of life." Keeping journals and recording important events, testimonies, and spiritual experiences creates a record for our posterity. It helps preserve the "welding link" between generations and can provide strength and faith to our descendants long after we are gone.

The salvation of my ancestors is essential to my salvation.

Discussion Point:

This section contains some of the most beautiful and powerful language about the work for the dead. Joseph Smith calls it the "most glorious of all subjects belonging to the everlasting gospel." He explains that without our ancestors, we cannot be made perfect; a "welding link" is required to connect the entire family of God. This work provides a "voice of gladness" for both the living and the dead, uniting generations through the sealing power of the priesthood. The early Saints felt this joy immediately, with Phebe Woodruff writing that the doctrine was "cordially received by the church and they are going forward in multitudes."

Doctrine and Covenants 128:18: "For we without them cannot be made perfect; neither can they without us be made perfect... a whole and complete and perfect union, and welding together of dispensations, and keys, and powers, and glories should take place..."

Discussion Question:

In verse 18, we learn that "we without them cannot be made perfect." In what ways is the work of salvation for our ancestors necessary and essential for our own salvation?

Possible Answers: God's plan is for families to be sealed together eternally. Our exaltation is tied to our ancestors as part of a great, interlinked family chain. By serving as proxies for them, we not only offer them salvation but also participate in the "great work" that purifies our own hearts, deepens our love for the Savior, and helps fulfill the purposes of God.

Summary of the Lesson

This lesson covers profound doctrines revealed during the Nauvoo period. It begins with the joyful introduction of baptism for the dead, a doctrine that brought immense hope to the Saints. It also teaches the importance of balancing Church service with the sacred duty to care for our families, as shown in the counsel to Brigham Young. Through Joseph Smith's example, we learn to find faith and joy even in the midst of severe trials. The central focus is on the glorious doctrine of salvation for the dead. The Lord revealed the specific, orderly way in which these ordinances must be witnessed and recorded, emphasizing that this work is essential for our own salvation, creating a "welding link" that connects the entire human family.

May your heart be filled with the 'voice of gladness' for His glorious plan.
