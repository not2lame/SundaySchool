A Discussion Guide for “A House unto My Name”

This guide is designed to help class members apply the principles from these sections to their own lives and to be better prepared to contribute to the class discussion.

Background: A New Beginning in Nauvoo

To appreciate the revelations in Doctrine and Covenants 124, it's essential to understand the context. The year is 1839. The Latter-day Saints are refugees. After enduring violent persecution, the Haun's Mill Massacre, and Governor Boggs's infamous "Extermination Order," they have been forcibly driven from Missouri.

Having lost their homes and property, they find temporary compassion among the citizens of Quincy, Illinois. Meanwhile, Joseph Smith and other leaders, who had been imprisoned in Liberty Jail, are allowed to escape their captors. The Church purchases swampy, mosquito-infested land along the Mississippi River. With characteristic faith, the Saints drain the land and begin building a new city, which they name Nauvoo, a Hebrew word meaning "beautiful."

It is in this setting of a fragile new beginning, after years of intense suffering, that the Lord reveals His will. He commands the Saints to once again build a temple—a holy house where He can restore crucial ordinances and endow His people with power, honor, and eternal life. This revelation is a powerful testament to the Saints' resilience and the Lord's forward-moving work.

I can invite others to come unto Christ.

Discussion Point:

One of the first commands in Nauvoo was for the Prophet to "make a solemn proclamation of my gospel" to the kings and rulers of the world. This was a bold declaration of the divinity and scope of the Restoration. This same work of proclamation continues today, as exemplified by "The Restoration of the Fulness of the Gospel of Jesus Christ: A Bicentennial Proclamation to the World," issued by the First Presidency and Quorum of the Twelve in 2020.

Discussion Question:

The Lord commanded Joseph Smith to write a proclamation in 1841, and the- modern Apostles issued another in 2020. What does this ongoing pattern of solemn proclamation teach us about the Lord's work and our role in it?

Possible Answers: It teaches that the Restoration is not a historical event but an ongoing process. It shows that the Lord wants His work declared boldly and openly to the entire world. Our role is to add our personal witness to these prophetic declarations, sharing the message of the Restoration "normally and naturally" with those around us.

I can be a disciple whom the Lord trusts.

Discussion Point:

The Lord takes time in this revelation to speak about specific individuals, highlighting the Christlike attributes He loves and trusts. He praises Hyrum Smith for the "integrity of his heart" and George Miller because he "hateth covetousness." The Lord expresses His love and trust for those who are faithful and pure in heart.

Doctrine and Covenants 124:15: "And again, I say unto you, blessed is my servant Hyrum Smith; for I, the Lord, love him because of the integrity of his heart, and because he loveth that which is right before me, saith the Lord."
Doctrine and Covenants 124:20: “And again, verily I say unto you, my servant George Miller is without guile; he may be trusted because of the integrity of his heart; and for the love which he has to my testimony I, the Lord, love him.”

Discussion Question:

The Lord praises both Hyrum Smith and George Miller for their integrity. What does it mean to you to have integrity of heart? How can we develop this attribute that the Lord loves so much?

Possible Answers: Integrity means being whole, undivided, and consistently true to our beliefs, both in public and in private. It's the alignment of our actions with our knowledge of right and wrong. We can develop it by making and keeping small promises to ourselves and to God, by being honest in all our dealings, and by choosing what is right even when it is difficult or unpopular.

The Lord wants me to welcome and accept others.

Discussion Point:

Alongside the command to build a holy temple, the Lord commanded the Saints to build the Nauvoo House—a hotel. This was not for their own use, but specifically to be a welcoming place for "strangers and the weary traveler." Its purpose was to provide a place where non-members and visitors could stay, feel welcome, and have a positive place to "contemplate the word of the Lord." This command reveals God's deep concern for those who are not of our faith and our responsibility to be open, accepting, and hospitable.

Doctrine and Covenants 124:23: "And it shall be a house for boarding, a house that strangers may come from afar to lodge therein; therefore let it be a good house, worthy of all acceptation, that the weary traveler may find health and safety while he shall contemplate the word of the Lord..."

Discussion Question:

The Nauvoo House was a physical building, but the principle behind it is spiritual. How can we apply the "spirit of the Nauvoo House" in our wards and branches today? What are some specific things we can do to make visitors, new members, and "strangers" feel genuinely welcome and safe among us?

Possible Answers: We can make a conscious effort to greet people we don't know at church. We can invite new members or visitors to sit with us. We can be patient and understanding with questions about our beliefs. We can create a non-judgmental atmosphere where people feel they can belong before they believe. Essentially, we can make our chapels and our homes places of refuge and welcome for all.

Building a House for Sacred Ordinances

Discussion Point:

The central command of this revelation is to build a temple in Nauvoo. The Lord explains that He has "always commanded" His people to build temples so He can reveal His hidden ordinances and endow them with power. Ordinances like baptism for the dead, which had been performed in nature, must now be performed in His holy house. The temple is essential for receiving the fullness of priesthood blessings and being crowned with "honor, immortality, and eternal life."

Doctrine and Covenants 124:39: "Therefore, verily I say unto you, that your anointings, and your washings, and your baptisms for the dead, and your solemn assemblies... are ordained by the ordinance of my holy house, which my people are always commanded to build unto my holy name."
Doctrine and Covenants 124:49: "Verily, verily, I say unto you, that when I give a commandment to any of the sons of men to do a work unto my name, and those sons of men go with all their might... and cease not their diligence, and their enemies come upon them and hinder them from performing that work, behold, it behooveth me to require that work no more at the hands of those sons of men, but to accept of their offering."

Discussion Question:

The Saints were commanded to build a temple in Missouri but were "hindered by their enemies." The Lord's words in verses 49-55 offer a reassuring message. How does this principle of the Lord accepting our willing offering, even when we are prevented from completing a commandment, bring you peace in your own life?

Possible Answers: This principle is deeply merciful. It shows that the Lord judges us on the desires of our hearts and our diligent efforts, not just on the final outcome, especially when circumstances are beyond our control. It can bring peace to those who want to serve a mission but can't for health reasons, those who desire to have children but are unable, or those facing any other righteous desire that is hindered.

Inspired Direction Through Patriarchal Blessings

Discussion Point:

After the death of Joseph Smith Sr., the first Patriarch to the Church, the Lord calls his son, Hyrum Smith, to that same sacred office. A patriarch is an ordained office in the Melchizedek P priesthood with the authority to give patriarchal blessings. This blessing is a unique gift for every worthy member of the Church, containing inspired counsel and direction from Heavenly Father.

Doctrine and Covenants 124:91-92: “... that my servant Hyrum may take the office of Priesthood and Patriarch, which was appointed unto him by his father, by blessing and also by right; That from henceforth he shall hold the keys of the patriarchal blessings upon the heads of all my people,”

Discussion Question:

If a friend asked you what a patriarchal blessing is, how would you describe it? Based on the lesson or your own experience, why does Heavenly Father want each of His children to receive one?

Possible Answers: A patriarchal blessing is like a personal letter from Heavenly Father or a chapter in your book of life. It declares your lineage in the house of Israel and provides inspired guidance, promises, and counsel specific to you. Heavenly Father wants us to receive one because He loves us and wants to give us a "glimpse of our eternal journey" and the direction we need to navigate life and return to Him.

Summary of the Lesson

After being driven from Missouri, the Saints began anew in Nauvoo, Illinois. In this revelation, the Lord commands them to proclaim the gospel to the world and, most importantly, to build a temple. This holy house is essential for restoring sacred ordinances like baptism for the dead and for endowing the Saints with power and the blessings of eternity. The Lord praises individuals for their integrity of heart and teaches that He mercifully accepts our offerings when we are hindered from obeying His commands. Finally, He reaffirms the sacred office of Church Patriarch, an office through which all worthy members can receive a patriarchal blessing containing inspired, personal direction for their lives.

May the Lord accept your offerings and guide you toward eternal life.
